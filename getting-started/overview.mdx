# Getting Started: Your Relocation Checklist

Before packing your bags, you need to handle several practical items. This section guides you through the essential steps.

## Your First-Month Timeline

<Accordion title="Before Arrival (1-2 months)">

**Research & Planning**
- [ ] Determine your visa/residency type (tourist vs. residence permit)
- [ ] Set a moving budget
- [ ] Research neighborhoods that fit your lifestyle
- [ ] Connect with expat communities (Facebook groups)
- [ ] Arrange initial accommodation (short-term rental for first month)

**Financial Preparation**
- [ ] Inform your bank of international move
- [ ] Understand exchange rates and currency strategy
- [ ] Budget for initial setup costs ($1,000-3,000 estimated)
- [ ] Plan health insurance

**Documentation**
- [ ] Gather required documents for residence permit
- [ ] Get translations of important documents
- [ ] Make copies (physical and digital)

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="First Week">

**Accommodation**
- [ ] Arrive and confirm rental
- [ ] Inspect property thoroughly
- [ ] Understand utilities and how to use them
- [ ] Get keys to property and mailbox

**Banking & Money**
- [ ] Locate nearest bank
- [ ] Open Turkish bank account (if planning to stay)
- [ ] Exchange some currency
- [ ] Test ATMs (know which ones work)

**Essential Services**
- [ ] Register with your embassy
- [ ] Locate nearest pharmacy
- [ ] Find grocery stores
- [ ] Introduce yourself to property manager/neighbors

**Phones & Internet**
- [ ] Get local SIM card
- [ ] Test mobile providers
- [ ] Set up home internet
- [ ] Inform contacts of new numbers

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="First Month">

**Residency Requirements**
- [ ] Start residence permit application process
- [ ] Schedule any required medical appointments
- [ ] Get police clearance if needed

**Banking & Finance**
- [ ] Complete bank account setup
- [ ] Set up international transfers if needed
- [ ] Arrange ongoing income (if working)

**Healthcare**
- [ ] Register with doctor/pharmacy
- [ ] Get health records translated if planning long-term
- [ ] Get insurance sorted out

**Social Integration**
- [ ] Attend expat meetups
- [ ] Join relevant groups/clubs
- [ ] Start language lessons
- [ ] Begin exploring neighborhood

**Household Setup**
- [ ] Arrange longer-term accommodation (if initial was short-term)
- [ ] Get utility accounts in your name
- [ ] Buy essential housewares
- [ ] Set up cleaning/maintenance contacts

</Accordion>

### Get Translations

For Turkish residency, you'll need Turkish translations of:
- Passport
- Birth certificate
- Marriage/divorce certificates
- University degrees
- Medical records (if planning long-term)

**Where:** Certified translator (tercüman) - $2-5 per page

<Callout type="tip">
**Pro tip:** Get translations done in Turkey (cheaper, easier for authorities to accept). Wait until after arrival unless you know you'll need them immediately for residence permit application.
</Callout>

## Common First-Month Challenges

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Communication">
    **Challenge:** No Turkish, limited English outside tourist areas
    
    **Solution:**
    - Get translation app (Google Translate, Microsoft)
    - Learn basic phrases (5-10 survival words)
    - Join language group immediately
    - Expats often help with translations
    - Most paperwork has English support at government offices
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Bureaucracy">
    **Challenge:** Turkish paperwork seems confusing
    
    **Solution:**
    - Hire a "fixer" (local helper) for $20-50 to help with documents
    - Visit government offices EARLY (before 11 AM)
    - Take a Turkish friend or pay translator
    - Patient/persistent attitude helps
    - Most expats report: "It's not that bad once you know the process"
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Finding Housing">
    **Challenge:** Rental scams exist; housing market is informal
    
    **Solution:**
    - Use established sites (sahibinden.com, airbnb for first month)
    - Meet landlord in person
    - Never wire money without confirmation
    - Get written agreement (even basic)
    - Use escrow for deposits if first-time
    - Network with expats for trusted landlords
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Banking">
    **Challenge:** Turkish IBAN requirements, transfer fees
    
    **Solution:**
    - Plan for $2-10 per international transfer
    - Batching transfers saves money
    - Turkish banks are reliable despite being annoying initially
    - Revolut/Wise cards help for some transfers
    - Most expats report: "It works fine, just different"
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Your Support Network

### Who to Connect With

**Official Support:**
- Your embassy (registration essential)
- Turkish Migration Authority (Göç İdaresi)
- Local government office (Muhtar - neighborhood chief)

**Community Support:**
- Expat Facebook groups (search "Bodrum Expats")
- Expat information services
- Your property manager/landlord
- Neighboring expats

**Professional Support:**
- Turkish lawyer (avukat) for legal matters - $30-50/hour
- Tax advisor (muhasebe) for tax/finance - $20-40/hour
- Real estate agent (emlakçı) for housing - usually get 5-6% of rent

### Key Expat Organizations

- **Bodrum Expats Network:** Main meetup group
- **British Expats Bodrum:** UK-focused group
- **Women expat groups:** Several active groups
- **Industry-specific groups:** Digital nomads, freelancers, retirees

**Recommendation:** Join Facebook groups BEFORE arriving. Post introduction, ask questions, get advice from people already there.

## When to Visit Before Relocating

If possible, visit for 1-2 weeks before permanently moving:

✓ Explore neighborhoods and visualize where you'd live
✓ Meet with property managers/landlords in person
✓ Test internet speed (critical if working remotely)
✓ Meet other expats and ask questions
✓ Understand transportation and local services
✓ Fall in love with the place (or realize it's not for you)

**Many expats report:** "Visiting first prevented a regretted move. The place needs to feel right."
